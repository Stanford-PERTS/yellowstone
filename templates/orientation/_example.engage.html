The Solution: The Engagement Project

The Engagement Project helps teachers:

Use fast student surveys to identify the causes of student disengagement

Learn evidence-based strategies for increasing student engagement

Track progress in order to find the most effective strategies for engaging their students

About the Program

Evidence-based

When teachers participate in The Engagement Project, they get access to cutting-edge resources and recommendations from leading behavioral scientists, including Carol Dweck, Camille Farrington, Dave Paunesku, Jason Okonofua, Greg Walton, and David Yeager. Best of all, teachers don’t have to take anyone else’s word for what works — they use a simple, but powerful, system to track what works for their students. Participating teachers get ongoing, confidential feedback so that they can iteratively test out and adapt evidence-based strategies to find the ones that work best for their students.

Teacher-Driven

The Engagement Project is driven by teams of teachers called Engagement Teams. Usually, an Engagement Team is comprised of 2–8 teachers. These teachers collaborate over the course of an Engagement Project Cycle, usually a 2–3 month period. During that time, they identify concrete, evidence-based ideas they can integrate into their own practice, undertake a systematic process for improving student engagement, and engage in productive, structured learning with colleagues.

The Process: What participation looks like

A group of teachers (usually 2–8 teachers) forms an Engagement Team. They commit to work together for at least one Engagement Project Cycle (usually 2–3 months). During the Cycle, they use Copilot, our anonymous survey platform, to track specific learning conditions that are affecting students’ engagement, like "Teacher Caring" and "Meaningful Work". Teachers get automated custom weekly reports describing students’ perceptions of the conditions in their classes. Then they work together to learn and test out different strategies for improving those conditions.

Improvement Cycle (4+ times)

Survey

Teachers use a short student survey to identify causes of student disengagement in their classes and track how their efforts to engage students change over time. Teachers receive confidential survey reports containing results for each of their participating classes and aggregated results for the team.

  • Survey Instructions
Team Meeting

Teachers meet to reflect on their survey results and to discuss evidence-based strategies to improve learning conditions in their classrooms. They share their experiences using and adapting the teaching strategies. In their final meeting, teachers review their progress, celebrate successes, and make plans for their next Engagement Project cycle.
